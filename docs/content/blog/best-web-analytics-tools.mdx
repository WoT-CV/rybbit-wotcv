## How to choose a web analytics tool

Four questions sort the whole market:

1. **Traffic stats or behavior?** Counting visits, referrers, and top pages is the easy half. Understanding *behavior* — funnels, session replay, user journeys, errors — is where tools diverge sharply. Simple trackers stop at the first half.
2. **Cookies or cookieless?** Cookie-based tools (GA4 chief among them) require consent banners in most of the world, and every declined banner is a visitor deleted from your data. Cookieless tools see everyone and need no banner.
3. **Cloud or self-hosted?** If analytics data legally can't leave your infrastructure, your shortlist is the open-source tools: Rybbit, Matomo, Plausible CE, Umami.
4. **Who reads the dashboard?** If the answer includes non-analysts, weight simplicity heavily. The most common analytics failure isn't missing data — it's a tool nobody opens.

## Google Analytics 4

**The default, and the tool everyone on this page is measured against.**

GA4 is free, integrates natively with Google Ads, and exports raw events to BigQuery — for paid-acquisition teams inside Google's ecosystem, it remains genuinely hard to replace. It's on this list on merit.

But treat its price with suspicion: "free" is funded by your visitors' data feeding Google's ad network. The operational costs are consent banners (and the 30–60% of traffic they cost you), report sampling on busy properties, 14-month standard retention, and an interface that has made "how do I just see my top pages?" a support-forum genre. Unsampled data means GA360, at roughly $50,000/year. If those are your pain points, our [detailed GA4 comparison](/compare/google-analytics) breaks down the switch — or see the full [guide to Google Analytics alternatives](/blog/best-google-analytics-alternatives).

## Plausible

**The standard-bearer for simple, privacy-first analytics.**

One page of stats, a tiny script (their claim: ~54× smaller than GA's), cookieless by design, EU-owned and EU-hosted. Plausible proved a paid GA alternative could be a real business, and a decade of polish shows. Its scope is deliberately narrow — traffic analytics, goals, basic funnels — which is either its best feature or its dealbreaker depending on your needs. **From $9/month** (10k pageviews), 30-day no-card trial, free self-hosted Community Edition. ## Matomo

**The open-source veteran with the largest feature list.**

Heatmaps, A/B tests, a tag manager, hundreds of plugins, and a free on-premise edition with unlimited hits — Matomo (ex-Piwik) is what "we replaced Google Analytics" meant for a decade, and it's still the compliance-department favorite (cloud data lives in Frankfurt, no sampling). The cost is a dated interface and pricing complexity on-premise, where advanced features come in paid plugin bundles from €275. **Cloud from €29/month** for 50k hits; no-card trial. [Rybbit vs Matomo →](/compare/matomo)

## PostHog

**The product-analytics suite that swallowed web analytics.**

PostHog bundles product analytics, session replay, feature flags, experiments, surveys, and a SQL-queryable event warehouse — with a web analytics dashboard on the side. For engineering-led product teams it's the most capable platform here, and the free tier is the market's most generous: **1M events every month, no card**. Past that it's usage-based (from $0.00005/event), which needs monitoring at scale. The trade: GA4-class complexity, because it's solving a much bigger problem than "how's my traffic." [Rybbit vs PostHog →](/compare/posthog)

## Fathom Analytics

**Simple stats from one of the original privacy-analytics companies.**

Cookieless, one clean dashboard, forever data retention, and at least 50 sites on every plan. Fathom's pitch is stability — a profitable independent company that's been doing exactly this since before it was a category. Pricing is a pageview slider (**$45/month at 500k pageviews**; smaller tiers cost less), 7-day trial, no free tier, no self-hosting. [Rybbit vs Fathom →](/compare/fathom)

## Umami

**Free, minimal, MIT-licensed — the developer's hobby-project default.**

Umami is the easiest analytics tool to self-host (a small Node app), and its cloud **Hobby plan is free forever**, with Pro at **$20/month for 1M events**. Reporting is deliberately minimal — pageviews, referrers, events — and that's the point. If your need is "decent stats on my side projects for $0," this is the answer. [Rybbit vs Umami →](/compare/umami)

## Simple Analytics

**The most privacy-conservative option, made and hosted in the EU.**

Simple Analytics collects the least data of any paid tool here and wears it proudly. In 2026 it's also carved out a clever niche: positioning itself as the *fix* for GA4's consent-loss problem (their number: GA can miss up to 60% of traffic). **Free for hobby sites** (5 sites, 1 month history); paid from **$20/month per user** with usage sliders; 14-day no-card trial. No self-hosting. [Rybbit vs Simple Analytics →](/compare/simpleanalytics)

## Pirsch

**The lowest-cost paid entry in privacy analytics.**

German-built, cookieless, with first-class server-side tracking SDKs and importers for GA, Plausible, and Fathom. **$6/month** buys 10k pageviews across up to 50 sites with unlimited team members and retention; $12/month adds funnels and A/B testing; 30-day no-card trial. Self-hosting exists but only behind the Enterprise tier. For tight budgets it's the best dollar-for-dollar deal on this list.

## Cloudflare Web Analytics

**Free and instant — as long as you understand what it isn't.**

If your site is behind Cloudflare, this is one toggle. It's cookieless and costs nothing, but Cloudflare's own docs note the dashboard is built on a ~10% sample of page-load events with roughly 30-day retention, and there are no goals, events, or funnels. Use it as a free second opinion, not as your analytics.

## FAQ

**What's the difference between web analytics and product analytics?**
Web analytics answers traffic questions (visitors, sources, pages); product analytics answers behavior questions (activation, retention, funnels) usually tied to identified users. GA4 and PostHog are product-analytics tools wearing web-analytics clothes; Plausible and Fathom are pure web analytics; Rybbit deliberately covers both sides for websites and apps.

**Are free web analytics tools good enough?**
Often, yes — Umami and PostHog's free tiers are real products, and self-hosting an open-source tool costs only your server. The free options to be wary of are the ones with invisible costs: GA4 (consent loss, complexity, your users' data) and Cloudflare (sampling, 30-day window).

**Can I run two analytics tools at once?**
Yes, and during any migration you should. Scripts from different vendors don't conflict, and a few weeks of parallel data is the only honest way to compare them. It's also the standard way to [leave Google Analytics](/blog/best-google-analytics-alternatives) without losing your bearings.

**Do cookieless analytics tools comply with GDPR?**
The cookieless tools here (Rybbit, Plausible, Fathom, Umami, Simple Analytics, Pirsch) are built to run without consent banners — no cookies, no cross-site identifiers, no personal profiles. Self-hosting adds the strongest guarantee, since data never touches a third party at all.
